## The most common benign cardiac tumour

Primary benign tumours of the heart are rare. The most common is **atrial myxoma** — literally a "myxoid tumour growing in the atrium."

About 75% of atrial myxomas arise in the left atrium and 20% in the right. Most are solitary; in familial forms, multiple lesions can occur.

## Symptoms

A classic triad:

### 1. Obstruction

The tumour can block blood flow at the mitral valve. This produces:
- Exertional shortness of breath
- Position-dependent fainting
- Pulmonary oedema
- Symptoms that ease lying down and worsen when standing

Because the tumour is mobile, symptoms can **change with body position** — a classic clue to atrial myxoma.

### 2. Embolism

Fragments break off and enter the circulation. The most common complication:
- Stroke (with left atrial myxoma)
- Acute peripheral arterial occlusion (e.g., leg ischaemia)
- Pulmonary embolism (with right atrial myxoma)

### 3. Systemic symptoms

Tumour-derived cytokines cause:
- Fever
- Weight loss
- Joint pain
- Fatigue
- Elevated ESR and CRP
- Sometimes mistaken for a viral or autoimmune illness

Often only one of these three patterns dominates — which is why diagnosis is sometimes delayed.

## Diagnosis

Typically two steps:

1. **Transthoracic echocardiography:** Identifies the tumour in over 95% of cases.
2. **Transoesophageal echocardiography (TEE):** Clarifies the attachment, stalk, and dimensions.

CT or MRI may add information, especially in older patients where coronary anatomy needs review in the same session.

## Surgery: the only option, with excellent results

Once an atrial myxoma is diagnosed, **surgical excision is recommended in every patient**. The reasons are:

- Risk of embolisation (a little higher every day)
- Risk of mechanical obstruction
- Rare risk of malignant transformation

Watchful waiting is not justified.

### Surgical plan

- Standard sternotomy or minimally invasive (right mini-thoracotomy) approach
- Heart-lung machine
- The tumour is excised with its stalk and a wide cuff of atrial wall
- The atrial defect is closed primarily or with a patch
- Coexisting mitral regurgitation (from mechanical obstruction) typically resolves

### Outcomes

- 30-day mortality: 1–2%
- Local recurrence: 1–3% (higher in familial forms)
- Long-term survival: similar to age-matched general population
- Hospital stay: 5–7 days

## After surgery

Annual echocardiography for the first 6–12 months for recurrence surveillance. After that, routine cardiac follow-up suffices in non-familial cases.

## Familial form (Carney complex)

Seen in 5–10% of cases. Multiple lesions, younger onset, skin pigmentation, and other endocrine tumours may co-occur. This form requires:

- Genetic testing
- Mandatory screening of first-degree relatives
- More frequent follow-up + earlier surgery
