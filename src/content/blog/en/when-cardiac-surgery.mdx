## Is surgery always required?

No. Modern cardiology can manage most heart conditions with medication. Cardiac surgery comes into play when **medication isn’t enough** or when there is **a structural problem**. This article clarifies the situations where surgery shouldn’t be delayed.

## Why does "delaying" matter?

Heart tissues undergo irreversible changes over time. A leaking valve left untreated for years lets the left ventricle dilate and lose function. After delayed surgery, the heart may not return fully to its earlier form. Timing is as critical as the surgery itself.

## Conditions where surgery should be considered

### 1. Coronary disease

When medication and stents are not enough, or with:
- **Left main disease** (especially > 50%)
- **Three-vessel disease** + diabetes
- **Complex anatomy:** long, calcified, multiple lesions
- **Failure of symptom control** with medication and stents

bypass surgery (classic or MIDCAB) is on the table.

### 2. Valve disease

- **Severe aortic stenosis with symptoms** (exertional fainting, chest pain, dyspnoea) — don’t wait
- **Severe mitral regurgitation with left-ventricular dilation**
- **Endocarditis** — particularly with large vegetations, embolic events, or developing heart failure

Modern guidelines now recommend earlier surgery even in asymptomatic severe aortic stenosis if left-ventricular function is starting to decline.

### 3. Aortic disease

- **Ascending aortic aneurysm ≥ 5.5 cm** (5 cm in Marfan; 4.5 cm in some cases)
- **Type A aortic dissection** (emergency)
- **Rapidly enlarging aneurysm** (> 0.5 cm per year)

### 4. Congenital heart disease

- Significant adult-detected left-to-right shunts (ASD, VSD)
- Surgery is most effective before pulmonary hypertension develops

### 5. Rhythm disorders

- **Long-standing AF unresponsive to catheter ablation** — MAZE
- AF detected during another cardiac operation — add MAZE

### 6. Other

- **Atrial myxoma** — surgery in every case
- **Constrictive pericarditis** — pericardiectomy

## Red flags: don’t delay

If any of these apply, request a cardiac surgical consultation **this week**:

- You’ve fainted with exertion
- You can’t sleep flat and need multiple pillows
- An echo shows severe aortic stenosis or marked valvular regurgitation
- An angiogram shows left main disease
- An aneurysm above 5 cm has been detected
- You’ve had prior cardiac surgery and new symptoms have developed

## Questions to ask before deciding on surgery

When talking with your doctor, don’t hesitate to ask:

1. "What happens if I don’t have surgery?"
2. "What are my survival numbers with surgery?"
3. "Which approach — minimally invasive or classic — fits me?"
4. "Can I get a second opinion?"
5. "How long does recovery take?"

A good surgeon answers clearly, doesn’t become defensive, and supports you in seeking a second opinion.

## Second opinions — a healthy step

For a decision as significant as cardiac surgery, a second opinion is reasonable and valuable. We see patients arriving with their existing treatment plans without judgement and review their cases with the same care. We treat second opinions as a natural part of communication.
