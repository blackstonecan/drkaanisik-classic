## Two approaches, the same goal

Coronary bypass surgery has one objective: open a new path beyond a blocked vessel. There are two main ways to get there.

In **on-pump bypass**, the heart is briefly stopped and a **heart-lung machine** takes over circulation. The surgeon sews into a still, motionless heart.

In **off-pump bypass** (beating-heart bypass), the heart keeps working. Specialised stabilisers immobilise only the small area being sutured. The body’s own circulation is preserved throughout.

## Why two methods?

Both have evidence-based roles. Choice depends on anatomy, comorbidities, and surgeon experience.

### Strengths of on-pump

- **Controlled field:** Operating on a still heart maximises suture precision.
- **Better in complex anatomy:** Multi-vessel disease benefits from unrestricted access.
- **Long-term patency data:** Decades of follow-up support its outcomes.

### Strengths of off-pump

- **Pump-free physiology:** Avoids the inflammation and embolic load associated with cardiopulmonary bypass.
- **Kidney-protective:** Reduces dialysis risk in patients with borderline renal function.
- **Neurological advantage:** Some studies show a small but meaningful drop in stroke rate.
- **Less transfusion:** No extracorporeal circuit means lower blood loss.

## Which method for which patient?

Roughly:

- **High-risk, multiple comorbidities:** Often off-pump.
- **Multi-vessel, complex anatomy:** Often on-pump.
- **Younger, lower-risk:** Both work well; surgeon experience tips the balance.
- **Calcified aorta:** "No-touch" off-pump avoids aortic manipulation.

## Common myths

> "Off-pump is less invasive."

No. Both approaches use the same sternotomy. "Minimally invasive" applies to techniques like MIDCAB, where the breastbone is preserved.

> "Off-pump is faster."

It depends on the surgeon. Single-graft cases are similar; multi-vessel off-pump cases can take longer.

> "The pump is always bad."

Modern heart-lung machines are not those of 30 years ago. In the right patient, on-pump doesn’t worsen outcomes.

## How is the decision made?

In consultation we review your angiogram, echocardiogram, and lab values together. The decision is shaped by:

- Number of diseased vessels and graft target quality
- Left ventricular function
- Renal function
- Aortic calcification
- Prior chest surgery

Nothing is "always better." What matters is choosing the right evidence-based approach for **you**.
