## Surgery isn’t mandatory — discipline is

The discovery of a small aortic aneurysm on imaging is initially worrying. The good news: most small aneurysms don’t require surgery for years. With the right surveillance and lifestyle decisions, things can stay stable for a long time.

This article is a roadmap for patients with an aneurysm that hasn’t reached the surgical threshold.

## Surveillance schedule

Frequency depends on size and location:

| Diameter (ascending aorta) | Recommended interval |
|---|---|
| 4.0–4.4 cm | Annual echocardiography |
| 4.5–4.9 cm | Annual echo or CT/MR |
| 5.0–5.4 cm | Every 6 months |
| 5.5 cm and above | Surgical consultation |

In Marfan or related genetic syndromes the threshold is lower (5 cm, even 4.5 cm). Growth of more than 0.5 cm per year shifts surgery forward.

## Blood pressure: the foundation

The single strongest factor influencing aneurysm growth is blood pressure. Targets:

- **General:** < 130/80 mmHg
- **Marfan or high risk:** < 120/80 mmHg

For control:

- **Beta-blockers** are first-line (reduce heart rate and aortic wall stress)
- **ARBs (e.g., losartan)** can add protection in Marfan
- **Diuretics and calcium-channel blockers** can help

Home monitoring: morning and evening, several times a week. Share consistent highs with your cardiologist.

## Exercise: yes, but the right kind

Living with an aneurysm doesn’t mean inactivity. **Regular aerobic exercise** actually slows aneurysm growth.

### Recommended

- Walking, hiking
- Swimming
- Light cycling
- Yoga (avoiding breath-holding and Valsalva manoeuvres)
- Light weights with high reps (under 5 kg)

### To avoid

- **Heavy weightlifting, bodybuilding** (heavy isometrics spike blood pressure)
- **Strength sports:** wrestling, powerlifting, heavy CrossFit
- **High-contact sports:** boxing, judo
- **Explosive sports:** sprinting, intense racket play
- **Diving (especially scuba)**

Rule of thumb: avoid activities involving breath-holding or sudden pressure changes.

## Smoking: must stop

Smoking directly damages the aortic wall structure and doubles the growth rate. It is the single strongest modifiable factor.

## Lifestyle

- **Mediterranean-style diet:** olive oil, fish, vegetables, fruit, nuts
- **Salt:** under 5 g per day
- **Alcohol:** moderate; avoid amounts that raise blood pressure
- **Stress management:** chronic stress raises BP; meditation and breathing exercises help
- **Sleep:** 7–8 hours; sleep apnoea must be treated (it accelerates aneurysm growth)

## Family screening

For first-degree relatives:

- If a parent, sibling, or child has an aneurysm history, **a one-time echocardiogram** is recommended
- Marfan diagnosis warrants genetic counselling and family screening

## Emergency awareness

Go to the **emergency department immediately** for:

- Sudden, severe "tearing" chest or back pain
- Sudden fainting
- Stroke-like symptoms (one-sided numbness, speech difficulty)
- Sudden weakening of the pulse in one arm

These suggest dissection or rupture. The decision to go is made in seconds, not minutes.

## The psychological side

Living with an aneurysm produces some anxiety in the first year. Most patients adjust over time. When you need it:

- Talk with a cardiologist or cardiac surgeon (seeing your numbers concretely helps)
- Join an aortic patient community (Marfan foundations, etc.)
- Seek a mental health professional if anxiety becomes pronounced
