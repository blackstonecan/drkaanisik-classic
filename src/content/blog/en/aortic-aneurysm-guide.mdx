## What is an aortic aneurysm?

The aorta is the body’s main artery — leaving the heart and travelling down through the abdomen to supply every organ. An **aneurysm** is a weakening of part of the aortic wall that balloons outward beyond its normal diameter.

By location:
- **Ascending aortic aneurysm:** Just above the heart.
- **Aortic arch aneurysm:** The curved section where the brain’s vessels branch off.
- **Descending thoracic aneurysm:** Within the chest.
- **Abdominal aneurysm:** In the abdomen.

This article focuses on **thoracic** aortic aneurysms.

## Are there symptoms?

Usually not. Aneurysms are typically found **incidentally** on imaging done for another reason (chest CT, echocardiogram).

When symptoms do appear, they may include:

- Deep, dull pain between the shoulder blades or in the front of the chest
- Hoarseness (an enlarging aorta can press on the left vocal nerve)
- Difficulty swallowing
- Aortic valve regurgitation symptoms (when the valve is also affected)

**Sudden, "tearing" chest or back pain** is always an emergency — it may indicate dissection or rupture.

## Risk factors

- High blood pressure (the strongest single factor)
- Smoking
- Family history (especially first-degree relatives with aneurysm)
- Genetic syndromes: Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos, Loeys-Dietz, Turner
- Bicuspid aortic valve
- Older age

## Screening: who needs it?

Anyone with a **first-degree relative** affected by thoracic aortic aneurysm should be screened once with echocardiography or CT. In Marfan and similar genetic conditions, annual surveillance is standard.

## Size and risk

Aneurysm diameter directly affects rupture risk:

| Diameter (ascending) | Annual rupture/dissection risk |
|---|---|
| < 4 cm | Very low |
| 4–4.9 cm | < 1% |
| 5–5.9 cm | 5–10% |
| ≥ 6 cm | 15%+ |

Surgery is generally recommended at **5.5 cm**; in Marfan or bicuspid valve cases the threshold drops to 5 cm or even 4.5 cm. Rapid growth (more than 0.5 cm per year) is an independent indication for surgery.

## Surgical options

By location and size:

- **Ascending aorta:** Replacement with a prosthetic graft. Bentall or David is added based on valve status.
- **Aortic arch:** Complex; often requires deep hypothermic circulatory arrest.
- **Descending thoracic:** TEVAR (endovascular stent-graft) or open surgery.
- **Abdominal:** EVAR (endovascular) or open surgery.

## Recovery

Open ascending aortic surgery typically requires 6–8 days in hospital. Full recovery takes 2–3 months. The graft itself is permanent and is monitored long-term with periodic echocardiography or CT.

## When surgery isn’t needed yet

For aneurysms below 5.5 cm, annual CT or MR follow-up is sufficient. During this period:

- **Blood pressure control** is critical (target < 130/80 mmHg)
- **Stop smoking**
- **Beta-blockers** are recommended in most patients
- **Avoid heavy isometric exercise** (powerlifting, bodybuilding)
- **Continue aerobic exercise** (walking, swimming — beneficial)
