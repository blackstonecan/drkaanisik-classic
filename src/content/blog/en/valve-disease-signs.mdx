## It starts quietly, then narrows your life

Heart valves keep blood moving in the right direction through the four chambers. A valve either narrows (restricting flow) or fails to close completely (allowing backflow). Either way, the heart’s workload rises.

The catch: valve disease usually progresses silently for years. Symptoms creep in gradually and are easy to dismiss as "ageing" or "being out of shape."

## Seven common warning signs

### 1. Shortness of breath on exertion

Stairs, slopes, or short walks that didn’t bother you before — now leaving you breathless? That’s the most common first sign of valve disease. As left-heart pressures rise, fluid backs up into the lungs.

### 2. Easy fatigue

Beyond ordinary tiredness — needing to sit down after shorter activities than before suggests reduced cardiac output.

### 3. Palpitations

An irregular heartbeat may be the first sign of atrial fibrillation. Mitral disease significantly raises AF risk.

### 4. Ankle and foot swelling

If your sock marks are deeper at night, or your shoes feel tighter — right-heart strain may be the cause.

### 5. Exertional chest pain or pressure

Classic in aortic stenosis. The cause isn’t the coronaries — it’s the heart muscle itself struggling to get enough blood.

### 6. Fainting or near-fainting (especially with exertion)

The most serious aortic-stenosis symptom. Don’t dismiss exertional fainting — it shouldn’t wait.

### 7. Breathlessness lying flat (orthopnoea)

If you can’t sleep flat and need extra pillows — left-heart failure is advanced.

## Which symptom suggests which valve?

| Symptom | Most often associated with |
|---|---|
| Exertional chest pain + fainting | Aortic stenosis |
| Breathlessness lying flat + ankle swelling | Mitral regurgitation |
| Palpitations + fatigue | Mitral disease |
| Dyspnoea + right-heart signs | Tricuspid regurgitation |

A single symptom never makes the diagnosis. Echocardiography clarifies which valve is involved and how severely.

## Murmurs — what they are and aren’t

Has your doctor heard a "murmur"? A murmur is the sound of blood crossing a valve or vessel. Not every murmur means disease:

- **Innocent murmur:** Common in children, pregnancy, fever.
- **Pathologic murmur:** Suggests valve narrowing, regurgitation, or congenital anomaly.

Any newly identified murmur should be evaluated with echocardiography.

## When to book an appointment

Don’t wait if you have any of:

- Marked fatigue with previously easy activities
- Recurring palpitations
- Exertional chest pain, pressure, or fainting
- New ankle swelling
- Breathlessness lying flat

Go to the **emergency room** for any of:

- Severe, persistent chest pain
- Sudden severe shortness of breath
- Sudden loss of consciousness

## How is it diagnosed?

Usually two steps:

1. **Clinical exam + ECG:** Murmur, rhythm, heart size
2. **Echocardiography:** Detailed valve structure, motion, and leak severity

In complex cases, transoesophageal echocardiography or cardiac MRI/CT may be added.

## Next step

If one or more of the symptoms above describe you, a simple echocardiogram usually answers most questions quickly. Don’t hesitate to book a check-up — the earlier valve disease is detected, the wider your surgical options remain.
