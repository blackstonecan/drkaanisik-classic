## Minutes matter, not hours

Aortic dissection is a tear in the inner layer of the aortic wall, allowing blood to flow between the layers. This creates a second channel running alongside the true lumen.

It splits into two types:
- **Type A:** Involves the ascending aorta — **emergency surgery**. Untreated mortality rises 1–2% per hour.
- **Type B:** Begins beyond the left subclavian artery — initially treated medically.

This article focuses on Type A — the form that is most rapidly life-threatening.

## Symptoms

The classic picture:

- **Sudden, "tearing" chest pain** — often radiating to the back
- Confusion (if brain perfusion is affected)
- Pulse loss in one arm or a pulse difference between arms
- Fainting
- Stroke-like symptoms
- Abdominal pain (if mesenteric arteries are involved)

Dissection is often confused with a **heart attack**. The pain character differs: heart attack pain is pressure-like and builds gradually; dissection pain is sudden and described as "tearing."

## Risk factors

- Uncontrolled hypertension (the most common single cause)
- Existing aortic aneurysm
- Genetic syndromes (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos)
- Bicuspid aortic valve
- Trauma (especially high-speed motor vehicle collision)
- Cocaine use
- Pregnancy (especially the third trimester in Marfan patients)

## Diagnosis

Suspected dissection:

1. Rapid **contrast-enhanced chest CT** — the gold standard.
2. Transoesophageal echocardiography (TEE) is a faster alternative when the patient is unstable.

If suspicion is strong, treatment is **started before imaging completes**.

## Emergency treatment

Type A → operating room immediately:

- **Target blood pressure < 110 mmHg** and pulse ~60 bpm (IV beta-blocker + nitroglycerine)
- Pain management (morphine)
- Blood products prepared
- Surgery typically under deep hypothermic circulatory arrest
- Ascending aorta replaced with a tube graft; valve and root addressed if needed

Surgery is complex and long (5–7 hours). Mortality correlates directly with time to surgery; survival is significantly higher in patients operated within 24 hours.

## ICU and beyond

Two to four days in intensive care, then a regular ward. Total hospital stay is typically 10–14 days. Recovery is longer than after standard cardiac surgery: full recovery takes 3–4 months.

## Long-term

Patients who have survived aortic dissection require:

- **Lifelong annual CT or MR** (remaining aortic segments may dilate over time)
- **Strict blood-pressure control** (< 130/80 mmHg, often < 120/80 is better)
- **Avoidance of heavy physical strain**
- **Genetic screening** for relatives, when appropriate

## When to think emergency

If you have **sudden, unusually severe chest or back pain**, do not attempt to manage it yourself — go directly to the emergency department. Even a one-hour delay can worsen outcomes.
