## Day one: intensive care

The first 12–24 hours after bypass surgery are typically spent in intensive care. During this time:

- Breathing is initially supported by a ventilator; in most patients the tube comes out within hours.
- Cardiac rhythm is monitored continuously.
- Chest drains (small tubes) prevent fluid build-up; they’re removed within 1–2 days.
- Pain is managed actively — modern protocols make this far more comfortable than people expect.

## Days 2–5: ward and mobilisation

Once you move from intensive care to a ward, mobilisation begins. The first goal: sitting at the bedside, then short walks in the corridor. Breathing exercises (incentive spirometry) are central — they significantly reduce lung complications.

Diet starts with fluids and progresses to normal food. Average hospital stay is 5–7 days; for minimally invasive procedures (MIDCAB, sutureless TAVI) it can be as short as 3–4 days.

## First two weeks at home

When you’re discharged, things won’t feel "normal" — and that’s expected:

- **Fatigue:** The most common complaint. Energy goes inward to heal. Short naps during the day are fine.
- **Sternum sensations:** After classic bypass, breastbone tightness and tenderness last 6–8 weeks. With MIDCAB, this is much milder.
- **Sleep disruption:** It takes a few days to leave hospital rhythm behind.
- **Mood:** Mild low mood or anxiety in the first 4–6 weeks is common. If it doesn’t lift, please tell us.

## Weeks 3–6: increasing activity

This is the period to gradually extend daily walks. The rule of thumb: walk at a pace where you can still talk without getting breathless.

- **Driving:** Wait 4–6 weeks after classic bypass; 2–3 weeks after MIDCAB.
- **Sexual activity:** Generally safe once you can climb a flight of stairs comfortably (around week 4).
- **Lifting:** Stay under 5 kg for the first six weeks. Strict adherence allows the sternum to fully heal.

## Weeks 6–12: back to work and life

- **Desk-based work:** Safe to return at week 6.
- **Physical work:** Reviewed at week 10–12 with your doctor.
- **Exercise:** Walking and low-intensity activity, building to normal exercise by month three.
- **Cardiac rehabilitation:** If offered, attend. Structured exercise, education, and psychological support measurably improve one-year outcomes.

## The long view: lifestyle

Bypass solves the blockage but doesn’t cure the underlying vascular disease. To keep your grafts patent for the long term:

- **Statins:** Almost always lifelong.
- **Antiplatelet therapy:** Aspirin is core; anything added is decided by your surgical team.
- **Smoking:** Must stop. The strongest single predictor of graft failure.
- **Blood pressure and glucose:** Stay on top of targets with your cardiologist.
- **Diet and exercise:** A Mediterranean-style diet and 150 minutes of moderate activity per week.

## When to seek emergency care

Don’t wait if any of these appear:

- New chest pain
- Severe shortness of breath
- Wound discharge, redness, or fever
- Sudden one-sided swelling (especially in a leg)
- Palpitations or fainting
