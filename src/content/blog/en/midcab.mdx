## Coronary bypass without opening the breastbone

In classic coronary bypass, the surgeon divides the breastbone (sternum) along the midline. **MIDCAB** — Minimally Invasive Direct Coronary Artery Bypass — skips that step. A 5–8 cm incision in the left chest wall, just below the breast, is enough.

The left internal mammary artery (LIMA) is sewn directly to the left anterior descending coronary artery (LAD), restoring flow past the blockage. In most cases the heart-lung machine isn’t needed; the operation is performed on a beating heart.

## Who is MIDCAB for?

MIDCAB isn’t the right choice for every bypass candidate. The patients we see the best results in tend to fit one of these profiles:

- **Single-vessel disease:** A significant blockage limited to the LAD.
- **Hybrid candidates:** Multi-vessel disease where MIDCAB to the LAD is combined with stenting (PCI) of the other vessels.
- **High open-surgery risk:** Older patients, lung disease, or prior chest surgery.

Every patient’s anatomy is different. The decision is made by reviewing your coronary angiogram, echocardiogram, and chest CT together.

## Advantages over classic bypass

- **Faster recovery:** Without a sternotomy, early-week pain is markedly less. Most patients return to full activity in 4–6 weeks; classic bypass takes 8–12 weeks.
- **Less blood loss:** A smaller surgical field reduces transfusion need.
- **Shorter hospital stay:** Typically 3–4 days, versus 5–7 for classic bypass.
- **Smaller scar:** Hidden under clothing — and quite cosmetic.

## Limitations

The biggest limitation of MIDCAB is access. The left chest incision exposes the LAD area directly, but circumflex or right coronary artery grafting requires a different technique. That’s when a hybrid surgical-interventional approach comes into play.

Surgeon experience matters. Working through a small incision is technically harder, so MIDCAB should be performed in high-volume, experienced centres.

## Operation day and after

Surgery typically lasts 2–3 hours. You spend a brief time in intensive care, then move to a regular ward. Drains are removed within 1–2 days; discharge is usually on day 3 or 4.

In the first week at home you avoid heavy lifting, driving, and long travel. Desk work resumes around week 6; physical work and exercise gradually return at 8–12 weeks.
