## Two classic procedures for the aortic root

The aortic root sits just above the heart and contains the aortic valve and the origins of the coronary arteries. When this region is diseased (dilation, aneurysm) and surgery is required, there are two principal options:

1. **Bentall procedure:** Replace the aortic valve, root, and ascending aorta together.
2. **David procedure:** Preserve the patient’s own aortic valve; replace only the root and ascending aorta.

Both are valid, evidence-based options. The choice depends on the valve’s condition and the patient’s anatomy.

## The Bentall procedure

Described in 1968 by Hugh Bentall, this procedure:

- Removes the diseased aortic valve
- Inserts a composite valved graft (a prosthetic valve combined with the aortic tube graft)
- Reimplants the coronary ostia onto the new graft

Result: a single replacement of valve + root + aorta.

### When is Bentall preferred?

- The aortic valve is significantly damaged (calcification, rheumatic damage, endocarditis)
- Bicuspid aortic valve with marked regurgitation
- The valve cannot be repaired due to a structural problem

### Advantages

- **Proven technique:** 50+ years of experience
- **Single-stage solution:** Valve and root together
- **Shorter operative time** than David

### Limitations

- **Mechanical valve:** lifelong anticoagulation required
- **Biological valve:** redo surgery often needed in 10–15 years
- Any future issue (e.g., endocarditis) affects the entire construct

## The David procedure

Described in 1992 by Tirone David, this technique:

- Leaves the aortic valve in place (when the leaflets are healthy)
- Replaces the diseased root and ascending aorta with a tube graft
- Re-suspends the aortic valve inside the new graft

Result: the patient’s **own valve is preserved**, only the root structure is renewed.

### When is David preferred?

- Root aneurysm with structurally healthy aortic leaflets
- Younger patients
- When anticoagulation is to be avoided
- Marfan syndrome (most of these patients are young)

### Advantages

- **No anticoagulation:** the native valve is preserved
- **Lower endocarditis risk**
- **Better hemodynamics:** natural valve flow pattern
- **Better long-term survival:** studies favour valve-sparing approaches

### Limitations

- **Technically more demanding:** surgeon experience matters
- **Longer operative time**
- **Valve regurgitation may develop:** 5–10% may need reintervention over years
- **Not suitable for everyone:** if leaflets are degenerated, David is not feasible

## How is the decision made?

Key factors in consultation:

| Factor | Favours Bentall | Favours David |
|---|---|---|
| Age | Older | Younger |
| Valve status | Marked damage | Leaflets healthy |
| Anticoagulation suitability | Acceptable | To be avoided |
| Genetic syndrome | Absent | Marfan, etc. |
| Surgeon experience | Standard | High-volume |

Often the final decision is made intra-operatively with TEE: if the leaflets are genuinely healthy, David proceeds; if they are not adequate, Bentall is performed. This safety net protects the patient regardless of scenario.

## Outcomes

Both procedures yield excellent results in experienced hands:

- **30-day mortality:** 1–3% (both procedures)
- **5-year survival:** 85–90%+
- **Reoperation:** Bentall depends on valve durability; David ~1% per year

## Next step

If you have aortic root disease and surgery is on the table, bring your imaging (CT angiography, echocardiogram) for a joint review. Whether your leaflets are repairable usually becomes clear quickly — and that answers the key question shaping the operative plan.
