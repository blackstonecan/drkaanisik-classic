## A heart in a "shell": what is constrictive pericarditis?

The heart sits inside a thin sac called the **pericardium**. Normally elastic and lubricating, it can — for a number of reasons — become inflamed, thickened, and stiff. The result is a heart effectively trapped inside an armored shell — it contracts but can’t fill properly.

The condition is called **constrictive pericarditis**. It looks like heart failure, but the heart muscle itself is intact. The problem comes from the "outer envelope."

## What causes it?

Most common causes:

- **Tuberculous pericarditis** (still important in many regions)
- **Prior cardiac surgery** (with subsequent pericardial thickening)
- **Prior radiotherapy** (especially chest radiation)
- **Autoimmune disease** (rheumatic disease, lupus, vasculitis)
- **Uraemic pericarditis** (advanced kidney failure)
- **Idiopathic** (no identifiable cause)

In Türkiye, tuberculosis remains a non-trivial cause; rates remain higher in regions with significant migration.

## Symptoms

The picture builds slowly over years:

- **Exertional shortness of breath**
- **Leg swelling, abdominal distention** (ascites)
- **Liver enlargement; jaundice in advanced cases**
- **Distended neck veins**
- **Early satiety, loss of appetite** (from liver and splenic congestion)

In advanced disease patients enter "cardiac cachexia" — losing muscle mass while retaining fluid. This is a late-stage finding and demands attention.

## Diagnosis: often difficult

Constrictive pericarditis isn’t straightforward to diagnose; it is often confused with **restrictive cardiomyopathy** (a primary heart-muscle disease). Reliable diagnosis combines:

1. **Echocardiography:** Pericardial thickness, septal motion patterns, abrupt early filling
2. **Cardiac MR or CT:** Pericardial visualisation (>4 mm thickness is typical)
3. **Right + left heart catheterisation:** Characteristic "square root" pressure tracings

If the diagnosis remains unclear, intra-operative biopsy can help.

## Pericardiectomy: removing the heart’s "shell"

Definitive treatment is surgical. The pericardium is:

- Removed via sternotomy (classic)
- Or via left thoracotomy (older preference)

Carefully separating the stiff, sometimes calcified pericardium from the heart muscle is technically demanding. Plaques adherent to the myocardium must be peeled away with care.

The extent removed:
- Anteriorly: to the sternum
- Inferiorly: to the diaphragm
- Laterally: to the left phrenic nerve (which is preserved)
- Posteriorly: as far as feasible on the right

## Outcomes

- **Immediate relief:** Most patients report marked improvement in breathlessness within hours of surgery
- **Ascites and oedema resolve:** within days
- **30-day mortality:** 5–10% (higher in advanced cases)
- **5-year survival:** 75–85%

The strongest determinant of outcome is **timing**. Operating before cardiac cachexia develops, and before liver and kidney are heavily affected, dramatically improves results.

## After surgery

- 7–10 days in hospital
- Normal activity by week six
- Full recovery by three months
- Diuretics tapered gradually
- Underlying causes (TB, rheumatic) treated separately

## Next step

Pericardiectomy should be on the agenda when:

- Unexplained chronic leg swelling + ascites
- Dyspnoea developing after prior cardiac surgery
- Tuberculosis history + heart-failure-like symptoms
- Heart failure developing late after chest radiotherapy
