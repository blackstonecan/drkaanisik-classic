## Mitral valve: repair or replace?

The mitral valve is a one-way valve between the left atrium and left ventricle. When it fails to close properly blood flows backwards (mitral regurgitation); when it narrows, flow forwards is impaired (mitral stenosis).

Treatment options split into two paths: **repair the existing valve** or **replace it with a new one**. The core principle of modern cardiac surgery: **repair, whenever possible**.

## Why repair first?

Preserving the patient’s own valve has measurable long-term advantages:

- **Better left ventricular function:** Native valve mechanics are preserved.
- **No anticoagulation:** A mechanical valve requires lifelong warfarin; repair usually does not.
- **Lower endocarditis (infection) risk.**
- **Better long-term survival:** Studies consistently show longer survival after successful repair than after replacement.

## When is repair feasible?

Success depends on the type of valve disease:

- **Degenerative MR (Barlow / FED):** Repair rate over 95%. Modern techniques (neochords, annuloplasty ring, triangular resection) are highly effective.
- **Functional MR (heart-failure related):** Possible but with more variable long-term results.
- **Rheumatic mitral stenosis:** Durable repair is rare; commissurotomy may be tried in selected cases.
- **Endocarditis:** Small vegetations — repair; widespread destruction — replacement.

## Repair techniques

Key surgical tools:

- **Annuloplasty ring:** Restores the dilated annulus to its anatomic shape.
- **Neochords (artificial chordae tendineae):** ePTFE strands replace ruptured or elongated native chords.
- **Leaflet resection / re-shaping:** Excess tissue on the posterior leaflet is removed.
- **Edge-to-edge repair:** In selected cases the two leaflets are joined.

## When is replacement necessary?

Replacement is the right choice when:

- Severe leaflet calcification
- Widespread rheumatic damage
- Extensive tissue loss after endocarditis
- An intra-operative TEE shows unsatisfactory repair (we then convert to replacement)

Two valve options:
- **Mechanical:** Highly durable (40+ years), but lifelong anticoagulation required.
- **Biological (tissue):** No anticoagulation needed, but may require redo replacement at 10–15 years. Preferred in older patients.

## Minimally invasive approach

Mitral surgery today is often performed through a **right mini-thoracotomy** — without dividing the breastbone. A 5–6 cm incision below the right breast is enough. The advantages:

- Less pain
- 4–5 day hospital stay
- Return to normal activity in 3–4 weeks

Repair success rates are equivalent to conventional sternotomy.

## Surgical timing

Repair gives the best results when the valve is still "salvageable." Waiting too long can cause irreversible left-ventricular damage. Modern guidelines recommend earlier surgery for symptomatic severe regurgitation — particularly when high repair success is expected.
