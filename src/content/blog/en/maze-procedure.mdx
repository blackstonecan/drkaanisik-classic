## A surgical solution for atrial fibrillation

**Atrial fibrillation (AF)** is the most common rhythm disorder worldwide. Its prevalence rises with age — over a tenth of people aged 65+ live with it. Symptoms include palpitations, fatigue, and exertional shortness of breath. The most serious complication is clot formation in the left atrium leading to **stroke**.

Treatment options:
1. **Drug-based rhythm/rate control** + anticoagulation
2. **Catheter ablation** (interventional)
3. **Surgical ablation — the MAZE procedure**

This article focuses on option 3.

## What is the MAZE procedure?

Developed in 1987 by Dr. James Cox, the MAZE procedure surgically blocks the abnormal electrical pathways that drive AF.

The original technique is called Cox-Maze; "maze" reflects the labyrinth pattern of incisions that leaves only a single permitted path from sinus node to AV node. All other electrical paths are interrupted.

Modern variants:
- **Cox-Maze IV:** Replaces incisions with radiofrequency or cryoablation lines.
- **Mini-MAZE:** Targets the posterior left atrium and pulmonary veins.

## Who is it for?

MAZE is most relevant in:

- **AF patients undergoing concurrent cardiac surgery:** Adding 30–45 minutes during a bypass, valve, or aortic operation.
- **AF patients in whom catheter ablation has failed**
- **Long-standing persistent AF** (where drugs and catheter ablation aren’t enough)
- **Enlarged left atrium** (typically > 5–5.5 cm)
- **History of clot-related stroke** — particularly when the left atrial appendage is also closed in the same operation

## Left atrial appendage — a bonus benefit

In MAZE the **left atrial appendage** (LAA) is also surgically closed. Around 90% of AF-related clots originate from this small pouch. Closing it markedly lowers stroke risk — independent of whether the ablation itself maintains sinus rhythm.

## How is it done?

When combined with another cardiac operation:
- Sternotomy or mini-thoracotomy
- Heart-lung machine
- Ablation lines placed in the left atrium during the main procedure
- LAA stapled or oversewn
- 30–45 minutes added to the operation

In stand-alone MAZE (for AF only):
- Right mini-thoracotomy (minimally invasive)
- Heart-lung machine if needed
- 2–3 hour operation

## Success rates

- **Concurrent MAZE:** ~75–85% sinus rhythm at one year
- **Stand-alone MAZE:** ~85–90% at one year
- **Five-year sinus rhythm after catheter ablation:** ~50%
- **Five-year sinus rhythm after MAZE:** ~75–85%

In other words, MAZE produces noticeably more durable results than catheter ablation.

## Afterwards

Atrial arrhythmias may persist for the first three months — this is the wound-healing phase. After three months, the rhythm is reassessed; if sinus rhythm is restored, anticoagulation can often be tapered (provided the LAA was also closed).

ECG and Holter monitoring at 3, 6, and 12 months, then annually.

## Risks

- Pacemaker need: 5–10% (ablation near the sinoatrial node)
- Bleeding: 2–3%
- Stroke (intra-operative): ~1%
- Wound infection

## Next step

If you’re scheduled for cardiac surgery and have AF, MAZE should always be on the agenda. Stand-alone MAZE is also an option for medication-resistant long-standing AF. Bring your treatment history and rhythm records to a consultation for a joint review.
