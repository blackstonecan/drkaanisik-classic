## An alternative to open surgery for aortic stenosis

**TAVI** — Transcatheter Aortic Valve Implantation — is a catheter-based valve replacement for severe aortic stenosis, performed without open-heart surgery. First introduced in 2002, it has become the standard of care for many older patients over the past two decades.

During the procedure a new biological valve, compressed inside a thin catheter system, is inserted via the femoral artery in the groin. It is advanced up the aorta, positioned inside the diseased valve, and expanded. The old valve isn’t removed — the new one starts working from inside it.

## Who is TAVI for?

In its early years TAVI was offered only to patients at very high surgical risk. Indications have widened considerably:

- **Patients over 75** with severe aortic stenosis (routine indication)
- **High surgical risk** (lung disease, kidney failure, frailty)
- **Prior chest surgery**
- **Selected younger, anatomically favourable** intermediate-risk patients (case by case)

The decision is always made by a heart team (surgeon, cardiologist, anesthetist, imaging specialist). Aortic annulus dimensions, calcification pattern, coronary ostia distance, and access vessel quality are reviewed in detail on CT angiography.

## TAVI vs. open AVR

| Feature | TAVI | Open AVR |
|---|---|---|
| Incision | Small puncture in the groin | Sternotomy |
| Anaesthesia | Often conscious sedation | General required |
| Hospital stay | 2–4 days | 5–7 days |
| Recovery | 1–2 weeks | 6–8 weeks |
| Valve durability (younger) | 8–12+ years | 15–20 years (bioprosthesis) |

## Procedure day

Most patients receive light sedation; general anaesthesia is available if needed. The procedure takes 60–90 minutes in the cath lab, with continuous fluoroscopy guiding valve positioning.

The first night is spent in a coronary care unit. Most patients stand the next day and are discharged within 2–4 days.

## Risks and limitations

TAVI is a high-success procedure but not without risk:

- **Pacemaker need:** The most common complication (5–15%). Some patients need a permanent pacemaker afterwards.
- **Vascular injury:** Femoral access may need surgical repair.
- **Stroke:** Low (2–4%) but a meaningful risk.
- **Paravalvular leak:** Small leaks between the new valve and the aortic wall — most resolve on their own or are well tolerated.

## Life afterwards

Antiplatelet therapy is taken for six months. Prophylactic antibiotics may be needed before dental work. Annual echocardiography monitors valve function.

Most patients feel a significant improvement in shortness of breath and chest pain — quality of life rises markedly.
