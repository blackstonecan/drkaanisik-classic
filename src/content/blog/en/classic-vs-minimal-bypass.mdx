## Same problem, different door

In coronary bypass surgery the goal is simple: restore blood flow past a blockage. **Classic bypass** and **minimally invasive bypass** (MIDCAB and similar variants) reach that goal through different doors.

## Classic bypass: the proven standard

In classic (sternotomy) bypass, the surgeon divides the breastbone in the midline. This access:

- Reaches every coronary artery directly.
- Is the safest approach in multi-vessel disease.
- In emergencies, gives the team rapid access to all instruments.
- Is supported by decades of long-term follow-up data.

When the aorta, valves, and multiple vessels need to be addressed in one session, classic bypass remains the gold standard.

## Minimally invasive: superior in the right patient

MIDCAB and similar techniques work through 5–8 cm incisions. The breastbone is preserved; the surgeon works between chest-wall muscles.

- **Faster recovery:** Most patients return to full activity in 4–6 weeks.
- **Less pain:** Sternum integrity intact means dramatically less early-week discomfort.
- **Cosmetic edge:** The scar sits beneath bra-strap or t-shirt line.
- **Shorter stay:** 3–4 days versus 5–7 for classic.

But minimally invasive isn’t for everyone.

## Which approach for which patient?

| Situation | Recommended |
|---|---|
| Single-vessel (LAD) disease | MIDCAB |
| Multi-vessel, complex anatomy | Classic bypass |
| Concurrent valve or aortic surgery | Classic bypass |
| Older age, multiple comorbidities, high open-surgery risk | Hybrid (MIDCAB + stent) or heart team review |
| Redo surgery (prior bypass) | Case by case; minimally invasive can be advantageous |

## Are long-term outcomes different?

In experienced hands, long-term outcomes are very similar between the two. With the right patient selection, five-year graft patency and survival rates are not clinically different.

The clear differences are in **early comfort** and **recovery speed**. For an active, working person — or someone with single-vessel disease — the minimally invasive advantages are hard to ignore.

## How decisions are made

At our practice in Antalya every case is, where possible, reviewed in a heart team huddle (surgeon, cardiologist, anesthetist). Imaging, labs, and the patient’s lifestyle priorities are discussed together.

Key questions:

1. **What does the anatomy permit?**
2. **Will any concurrent surgery be needed?**
3. **What are the patient’s personal recovery goals?**
4. **Do comorbidities limit the minimally invasive option?**

## The wrong question: "Which is better?"

The right question is: "Which is better **for me**?" — and that answer is patient-specific.

Bring your angiogram report to a consultation and we’ll walk through every option clearly. The decision is always yours; our job is to lay out the choices honestly.
